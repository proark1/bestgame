The Tale, in Strips

Swipe through the story of how the kingdom split, and the four heroes who took its place.

"The garden is not a garden. The garden is a continent under a sun that does not know our names. We were one queen. Now we are four crowns, and the war is teaching us how to count." — fragment, scratched into a sugar-vault wall, author unknown

One backyard. One continent.

Everything in Hive Wars happens in a single overgrown garden — loose stones, roots, leaf litter, a cracked terracotta pot, a dripping hose. From a human window it's a patch of neglect. From the ants' angle it's a continent, and every tile in it has been walked across, fought over, and remembered.

Two layers cut through the world. The Surface is sun, dew, birdsong, and the long threat of the gardener's boot. The Underground is humid, glowing with fungus, always warm — where larvae hatch and the throne keeps its heart. Only the Queen Chamber bridges both. Lose it, and your story ends there.

The Long Cold broke the kingdom.

Three winters ago a frost came down through the garden and did not lift for a full season. Collectors froze on their stems. Tunnel junctions collapsed under thaws that never came. The old single kingdom — one queen for every creature in the yard — broke with the ice.

Smaller colonies emerged. Each crowned a new queen. Each claimed to be the true continuation of the old throne. What had been ritual became politics; what had been family became rivals. The Hive Wars are the long bloom of that fracture, and you have inherited a chamber on the day the war is asking its loudest question.

Four factions. One pheromone alphabet.

The ancient alliance survived as four factions. They share a pheromone alphabet inherited from the pre-Cold colony — and a grudge — but each lives and fights very differently. The Ants are the only side that fields buildings; everyone else is a mercenary, signed in sugar and silk.

The Wasp Court sells air support from two fence panels over. The Stone Orchards — beetle country — trade carapace for ore. The Silk Court spiders speak in thread, not scent, and are the most expensive sword in the yard. Your raid deck is whoever you can afford this season.

You draw the song.

The "draw a path" gesture you'll learn in your first raid is the player's queen composing in pheromone. A raid is a scripted song: a sequence of trails, each one carrying a unit type, a priority, a destination. Mercenaries follow because they're paid to. Spiders follow a silk bead laid along the same line.

If your queen chamber falls, the trails go silent. The mercenaries walk home. The raid ends. So every tile you place on defense, and every breath of pheromone you spend on offense, is the same answer to the same question: which crown survives the spring?
