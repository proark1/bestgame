# Hive Wars — World & Story

A lore bible for the backyard. Every codex blurb, card power line, and
faction name currently in the game is kept coherent here. Anyone adding
a new unit, building, or quest should crack this open first — the
cosmology is tight, and the art pipeline reads best when the writer and
the painter share a map.

The opening narrative — **The Tale of the Four Crowns** — is the in-
universe hook. Every named unit, building, and hero earns a beat. Use it
as the marketing trailer copy, the chest-opening cinematic spine, and
the canonical answer to "why does any of this matter?"

---

## The Tale of the Four Crowns

> *"The garden is not a garden. The garden is a continent under a sun
> that does not know our names. We were one queen. Now we are four
> crowns, and the war is teaching us how to count."*
> — fragment, scratched into a sugar-vault wall, author unknown

### Cold open — Three nights before the Long Cold breaks

The frost has held for ninety days. In a cracked terracotta pot at the
edge of the world, a **Queen Chamber** glows from within — a single
warm coal in a continent of white. Her surface turret is dark. Her
underground throne is not.

A **Worker Ant** climbs a frozen stem with a leaf folded twice over her
back. She is the last forager out tonight; everyone else gave up at
sundown. Her sister waits inside the **Dew Collector** at the base of
the stem, palms cupped, ready to catch what the leaf brings down.

Above them both, somewhere up where the stars hang too still, a
**Dragonfly** is circling. Watching. Counting.

The leaf reaches the collector. The dew falls. The sister smiles.

The dragonfly stoops.

And the swarm comes.

### Act I — The Bladewidow comes home

The **Praying Mantis** they would later call the **Bladewidow** is
not, that night, a hero. She is a tired duellist with a torn silk
scarf and two scythes she has not cleaned since the eastern raid. She
arrives at the gate of an unknown colony because her own queen is
dead, her own chamber is rubble, and the pheromone trail to the next
nearest garden led here.

She does not knock. She steps over the **Leaf Wall**, ducks under the
**Thorn Hedge**, and lands in the courtyard between a sleeping
**Pebble Bunker** and a humming **Mushroom Turret** that swings to
track her on instinct, then — recognising no scent of war — pivots
politely back to neutral.

Inside, three **Soldier Ants** stand in a triangle around the chamber
mouth, jaws set. The youngest opens her mouth to challenge.

The Bladewidow lifts one scythe — slow, ceremonial — and lays it flat
across her own throat in the old gesture: *I am yours. Use me.*

The queen, watching from the throne below, says only: "Feed her."

A **Forager** brings her honey-dew in a stitched petal cup. A **Larva
Nursery** worker brings her a fresh blanket of spun root-fibre. The
Mantis sleeps for the first time in eight days. Outside, the dragonfly
wheels once more, sees nothing it likes, and rides the night wind back
to whoever sent it.

When she wakes, a war begins.

### Act II — The Bulwark, who would not move

The first raid arrives at noon, from the eastern tunnels — the **rival
beetle colony** that holds the ridge. Their swarm is honest about
itself: a wall of carapace, hauling momentum. **Shield Beetles** in
front, **Rollers** spinning up behind, **Bomb Beetles** giggling
nervously in the rear, fuses already cut short.

The colony's outer **Pebble Bunkers** take the first salvo. Two of
them crack. A **Termite** the rivals brought along gets to work on the
**Sugar Vault** wall — chrome mandibles, hungry sawdust raining — and
behind him a column of enemy **Worker Ants** are already moving up to
loot what spills.

This is the day the **Hercules Beetle** chooses a side.

He has been sleeping in a stone orchard north of the flagstone path
for three winters. Older than most queens. His carapace remembers a
**Mushroom Turret** salvo that other beetles only know from songs. He
walks — slow, the ground shaking on the *off-beat* of his step —
through the breach in the leaf-wall, plants himself in the gap where
the **Pebble Bunker** used to stand, and refuses, on principle, to be
a door anyone can walk through.

The lavender shimmer rolling off his shoulders is not magic. It is the
oath he is keeping. Every soldier within three tiles of him stands a
little taller, takes a turret hit a little better, finishes a fight a
little later than they otherwise would have.

The rival roller crashes into him at full momentum.

The roller stops.

The Hercules Beetle does not.

By dusk the eastern push is broken, the **Bomb Beetles** are scattered
giggling into the underground, and the colony's queen calls her
generals to the chamber. The Bladewidow is one. The Hercules Beetle —
**the Bulwark** — is the other.

Two crowns. The colony exhales for the first time in a season.

### Act III — The Honey Sovereign defects

She does not arrive at the gate. She arrives at the *roof* of the
**Queen Chamber**, hovers there with four golden wings spread wide,
and lays a crown of amber-and-pearl down on the surface tiles like a
calling card.

The **Wasp Queen** has been a sovereign-in-exile for two months. Her
elders refused to back her run on a rival mortar nest; she lost a
sister to that refusal. The elders thought the matter closed. She has
been carrying the closed matter in her head for eight weeks, and last
night, two fence panels over, she made her decision.

She defects with a hive's worth of grudges and a single droplet of
glowing nectar in her front legs.

The colony's regular **Wasp** snipers — mercenaries, paid in sugar —
hover at the edges of the courtyard, uncertain. The colony's
**Foragers** look up at her like they're seeing a queen for the first
time. (They are.)

She lands. She kneels. She presses the nectar drop to the wound on a
**Soldier Ant** who took the worst of the eastern push, and the wound
closes the way a flower closes at dusk.

Three crowns.

The **Aphid Farm**, never previously a strategic structure, becomes
overnight the colony's most-defended building — because the Honey
Sovereign demands fresh **aphid milk** for her healing draughts, and
every forager wants to be the one who brings her a cup. The **Leaf
Silo** doubles its leaf throughput in a week. The **Milk Pot** is
ringed with **Spore Towers** for the first time in its history.

The colony has a healer now. Everything else is downstream of that.

### Act IV — The Wallbreaker chooses the queen

He comes to the gate openly. **Stag Beetle**. Old enough to have
helped draw the original boundary line. War-hammer slung diagonally
across a back so wide it casts its own shadow. A circle of soldiers
forms around him at the courtyard, jaws bared on instinct, and he
does not even look at them — he looks past them, at the Queen
Chamber, at the queen herself, and he says one word: *"Where?"*

He doesn't mean *where to sit*. He means *where do you want me to
break the wall.*

The fourth crown is the simplest of the four. He hits buildings. He
hits them harder than anyone in the yard. The little crackling red
energy motes that dance around his mandibles are not a power he uses
on living things — they're the weight of the doors he's already
broken talking back.

The queen names him the **Wallbreaker**. She gives him a
**Tunnel Junction** of his own, on the eastern edge, with a mouth that
opens straight at the rival beetle ridge. He sleeps next to it like a
dog at a fire.

Four crowns. The war can begin.

### The marching season

Spring arrives in the garden — the gardener has not been seen for a
fortnight — and the colony fields its first true raid in three years.
The deck is the war:

- The **Bladewidow** drops first, on a tight pheromone trail, and is
  through the rival's outer **Leaf Wall** before the **Acid Spitters**
  on the ridge have finished tracking. Her aura makes the **Soldier
  Ants** behind her swing 25% faster, and the rival's first turret
  line falls before its second salvo.
- The **Bulwark** plants himself two tiles inside the breach. The
  swarm files past him — **Worker Ants** with leaves, **Foragers**
  with empty satchels, a single **Shield Beetle** mercenary keeping
  the right flank — and his shimmer makes every one of them harder to
  kill on the way past.
- A **Dirt Digger** column splits off, drops through a crack in the
  surface, and surfaces *behind* the rival **Sugar Vault**. They are
  carrying **Termites**. The vault wall starts singing the
  particular high note of mandibles meeting bark.
- A **Web Setter** lays a slowing thread across the rival's main
  avenue, and a **Roller** crashes into the **Honey Tank** that comes
  to clear the thread — both go down in a tangle, and the rival's
  mid-line is already a step out of position.
- An **Ambusher** comes up from a half-leaf cloak inside a tunnel
  the colony was not supposed to know about — a **Spider Nest** her
  handlers had seeded last winter, dormant until the silk thread
  pulled — and she takes the rival's queen guard from behind in
  perfect silence.
- A **Jumper** vaults the inner **Thorn Hedge**, lands on a
  **Mushroom Turret** roof, and is already at the chamber mouth
  before the turret has decided what to track.
- The **Fire Ant** column sets the **Aphid Farm** alight; the rival
  loses its milk in the first minute of the raid.
- The **Wasp** snipers — the regular mercenaries, not the queen — cut
  a corridor in the air for a flight of **Dragonflies** the colony
  has been quietly breeding for a month, and the dragonflies dive on
  the rival's **Hidden Stingers** before they can spring.
- A **Scarab** the rival queen had been saving for emergencies enters
  the field and is killed in seven seconds; from her shell crawls a
  litter of **Mini Scarabs**, who scatter in every direction and whom
  the colony's **Nest Spiders** — defending the home tunnels back at
  base — never get a chance to taste, because they're not the ones
  on this front.
- The **Honey Sovereign** stays high, follows the swarm, drips honey
  on every wound. A **Bomb Beetle** the rivals have left guarding the
  inner courtyard takes one look at her halo and detonates *prematurely
  out of nerves*, killing nothing but a **Root Snare** and his own
  fuse.
- The **Wallbreaker** comes last, walks past the burning **Aphid
  Farm**, past the toppled **Spore Tower**, past the cracked
  **Pebble Bunker**, past the slumped **Honey Tank** corpse, and he
  reaches the rival **Queen Chamber** at exactly the moment his
  +20% building-damage aura overlaps with a column of the colony's
  **Termites**.

The chamber falls in eleven seconds.

The rival queen, knowing the rules of the old single kingdom, lays
her seal-ring at the Wallbreaker's feet. He picks it up. He does not
crush it. He takes it back, three days later, and lays it on the
colony's queen's throne.

One war is over. The next four start that night.

### The slow truth

The frost did not break the kingdom. The kingdom broke the kingdom.

What the four heroes know — what the queen now knows, what the
**Honey Sovereign** flew across two fences to say out loud — is that
every one of the four crowns is a piece of the *same* old crown,
broken three winters ago in the chamber where a hairline crack still
runs the length of the throne. The Bladewidow is the precision of it.
The Bulwark is the patience of it. The Honey Sovereign is the mercy
of it. The Wallbreaker is the hand of it.

Whether the four can be welded back into one — whether the four
queens of the four colonies of the four corners of the garden can
ever again sit at one chamber and eat one dew at one dawn — is the
question the war is asking.

The player is the answer it gets.

## The World — One Backyard

Everything in Hive Wars happens in a single overgrown garden: loose
stones, roots, leaf litter, a cracked terracotta pot, a dripping hose.
From a human window it's a patch of neglect. From the ants' angle it's
a continent.

Two layers cut through the world:

- **Surface** — sun, dew, birdsong, the long threat of the gardener's
  boot. Where walls are built and turrets bloom.
- **Underground** — humid, glowing with fungus, always warm. Where
  larvae hatch, the throne keeps its heart, and tunnels braid the map.

The **Queen Chamber** is the only structure that bridges both — a
surface turret wired to an underground throne. Every colony has one.
Lose it, and the story ends there.

## The Long Cold

Three winters ago a frost came down through the garden and did not lift
for a full season. Collectors froze on their stems. Tunnel junctions
collapsed under the weight of a thaw that never came. The old single
kingdom — one queen for every creature in the yard, a peace of sorts —
broke with the ice.

Smaller colonies emerged, each with a new queen, each claiming that
their line was the true continuation of the old throne. What had been
ritual became politics. What had been family became rivals. The
**Hive Wars** are the long bloom of that fracture.

## The Four Factions

The ancient alliance survived as four factions. They share a pheromone
alphabet (inherited from the pre-Cold colony) and a grudge, but each
lives and fights very differently.

### Ants — the Hosts

The player. A single queen. A dual-layer base. Buildings, income, walls,
the works. Every **Ant** in the codex is either part of the colony
proper — worker, soldier, digger, forager — or a defensive structure
grown from colony materials (leaf, pebble, spore, honey-dew).

The ants are the only faction that fields buildings. Everyone else is a
mercenary.

**Named ants:**

- **Worker Ant** — the backbone. Not a soldier by training. Runs through
  flame if the pheromone trail says so.
- **Soldier Ant** — heavy-jawed, bred for the breach. Takes a turret
  hit so three workers can reach the vault.
- **Dirt Digger** — a worker who got lost one winter and came back
  wearing the tunnels like armor. Ignores surface walls.
- **Forager** — reads a garden better than a map. Goes for dew, leaf,
  anything sweet. Trophies come second.

### Bees — the Wasp Court

The **wasp court** is a foreign power. Their hive sits two fence panels
over, across the neighbor's gutter. They sell air support in exchange
for sugar the ants can barely spare. No one trusts a wasp. Everyone
wants one on their side.

**Hired by the ants:**

- **Wasp** — flying sniper. Glass cannon. Cuts a path over a defensive
  maze for an infantry line behind her.
- **Honey Tank** — three drones fused into one honey-soaked husk.
  Absorbs fire. If a honey tank reaches the vault, the raid is over.

### Beetles — the Stone Orchards

The **stone orchards** lie on the far side of the flagstone path. Where
the ants farm dew, the beetles farm ore. They arrived at the gates
after the Cold with carapaces they'd sharpened on pebbles for
generations and a proposition: hire us, or fight us. The ants hired
them. Most of them, anyway — a **rival beetle colony** still holds the
eastern tunnels and is the primary opponent in the bot raid.

**Hired by the ants:**

- **Shield Beetle** — forged carapace. Walks into the line of fire so
  the colony behind can work. Reduces turret damage around it.
- **Bomb Beetle** — explosive sacs on her back. Knows she only walks
  one trail. Grandma said aim for the biggest wall and don't flinch.
- **Roller** — tucks into a sphere and picks up speed on downhill
  trails. The longer the path, the harder she hits.

### Spiders — the Silk Court

The **spiders** do not speak the pheromone alphabet. They signal with
silk. Eight-legged, eight-eyed, and unfailingly polite at the
negotiating table, though no one is sure whether the politeness is
ritual or threat. They're the most expensive mercenaries in the yard.
They're also the best.

**Hired by the ants:**

- **Jumper** — crosses leaf walls like the walls are suggestions. Still
  hates pebble bunkers.
- **Web Setter** — lays silk as she walks. Anything in the thread
  slows to a crawl — useful when you want mushroom turret salvos to
  hit your own heavies by mistake.
- **Ambusher** — arrives in silence, leaves in silence. The queen
  never sees her come.

## The Pheromone Trail

The in-game "draw a path" gesture is the player speaking in pheromone.
Lore-wise, a raid is a **scripted song**: the player's queen composes a
sequence of trails for mercenary squads to follow, each trail carrying
a unit type, a priority, a destination. The mercenaries follow the
scent because they are paid to, not because they were born to. Spiders,
who don't read pheromone natively, follow their handler's silk bead
laid along the same line.

This is why your ants can deploy beetles, bees, and spiders from the
same deck: every unit on the field is reading (or approximating) an
**ant-made trail**. If the queen chamber falls, the trails go silent,
the mercenaries walk home, and the raid ends.

## The Buildings

The ant colony's buildings are grown, not built. Each one is a
biological machine with a particular role:

- **Queen Chamber** — the only two-layer structure. Surface turret
  rooted into an underground throne room. Tier gates every other
  building.
- **Dew Collector** — morning-dew petals piped down to the vault.
  Sugar income.
- **Larva Nursery** — warm, humid, softly glowing. New workers by the
  hundred. Underground only.
- **Sugar Vault** — storage cap. The deeper the vault, the thicker the
  armor.
- **Mushroom Turret** — a bioengineered puff-cap that spits spore darts
  at anything on a pheromone trail. The alchemist worker who bred it is
  remembered in every colony's poem.
- **Leaf Wall** — tightly folded chlorophyll armor. Cheap. Meant to
  funnel, not stop.
- **Pebble Bunker** — hauled from the creek bed by generations of
  workers. Built to outlast the colony that built it.
- **Tunnel Junction** — where surface meets underground. Workers pass
  freely in peacetime; in a raid, diggers use it to flank upward.
- **Dungeon Trap** — a sprung plate over a dart-vine shaft. Every
  raider thinks they know the layout. Every raider is wrong once.

## The Comic — "Three Springs Ago"

A short, six-panel sketch of the event that kicked off the current war.
Art can be generated from these beats in the same menuUi admin pipeline
as the card frame; they're sized 1:1 to a card portrait cell.

1. **The Last Warm Morning.** The old queen on the surface, eating
   dew. Her daughters at her feet. Sunlight on the terracotta pot.
2. **The Cold.** A single line of frost creeping across the garden.
   No living figures — just white.
3. **The Throne Splits.** Inside the underground chamber, a hairline
   crack runs the length of the throne. Two daughters staring at each
   other across it.
4. **The Wasp at the Gate.** A wasp hovering at the chamber mouth,
   one leg held out, holding a contract. The queen's guards on either
   side, jaws tense, unmoving.
5. **The Pact.** An ant queen and a beetle chieftain pressing seal-
   rings together over a leaf, silhouetted against a torch. A spider
   in the corner of the frame, watching, silent.
6. **The First Raid.** An opposing hive in the distance. The player's
   queen standing at her chamber mouth, pheromone whorls rising from
   her antennae, the rival base visible through the leaf-fringe.

## Writing style guide

When you add a new codex entry:

- **Role** — three to five words, present tense, verb-forward. ("Burst
  assassin", "Ranged defense", "Cheap blocker".)
- **Story** — 2–3 sentences. Anchor the unit to the world (a place,
  a season, a relationship). Don't explain stats. Let voice carry the
  faction.
- **Power** — 1–2 sentences. State the mechanical edge and one usage
  hint. Never contradict the live sim stats — this is the player's
  in-fiction reference, not marketing copy.
- Avoid naming the *player*. The codex is in-universe; it's written by
  the colony's scribe, not the game designer.

## Game-design review (as of main @ ce0fd79)

Taking stock of whether the systems currently wired in *make sense* and
*feel fair*, reading from `shared/src/sim/stats.ts`, `RaidScene.ts`'s
hardcoded bot, and the codex powers.

### What works

- **Dual-layer base** is the single strongest idea: it gives tunnel
  junctions, dirt diggers, and the queen chamber real mechanical
  meaning that the lore also leans on.
- **Deterministic sim + seed 0xc0ffee** means every raid is
  reproducible. Replays and raid-submit round-tripping benefit.
- **Power-line taxonomy** — role / story / power — reads cleanly on
  a card and gives the writer a 3-slot budget per unit. Easy to keep
  consistent.
- **Mercenary framing** justifies the mixed unit deck (ants + beetles +
  bees + spiders) without a Frankenstein roster. Cosmetically and
  narratively clean.

### Balance observations

Pulled from `UNIT_STATS`, DPS = `attackDamage × 30 / cooldownTicks`:

| Unit | HP | DPS | Range | Notes |
|------|----|-----|-------|-------|
| WorkerAnt | 20 | 3.3 | 0.6 | intended swarm; fine |
| SoldierAnt | 40 | 7.5 | 0.8 | solid anchor; fine |
| DirtDigger | 30 | 4.0 | 0.5 | utility (dig); fine |
| Forager | 15 | 3.0 | 0.5 | glass; fine if loot multiplier real |
| Wasp | 25 | 5.0 | **2.5** | flying + range 2.5 is the map; may be over-tuned vs turrets |
| HoneyTank | 80 | 6.7 | 0.8 | tank; fine |
| ShieldBeetle | 120 | 4.0 | 0.6 | tank; aura (per codex) not yet visible in stats.ts |
| BombBeetle | 30 | — | 0.4 | one-shot 25 dmg; fine |
| Roller | 50 | 6.7 | 0.7 | needs long trail; fine |
| **Jumper** | 35 | **9.5** | 0.6 | highest sustained DPS **plus** wall-hop. Likely dominant. |
| WebSetter | 25 | 0.75 | 2.0 | pure utility; fine |
| **Ambusher** | 30 | 12.0 | 0.7 | highest DPS **plus** first-strike-triple per codex. Explosive vs queen. |

Two outliers flagged:

1. **Jumper** is currently the highest-DPS ant-recruitable unit **and**
   it ignores leaf walls. The codex tries to balance that with "medium
   HP, medium damage — value is positioning" but the numbers don't back
   it up. Options: drop base DMG to 5, *or* lower HP to 25 so
   turret focus fire punishes the skip.
2. **Ambusher** with a triple-damage first strike is effectively 36 HP
   on target on first hit, which one-shots most turrets and half-kills
   the queen chamber off a single deploy. Pair the triple clause with
   a cooldown (e.g. only once per raid) or scope it to *non-queen*
   targets.

Neither bonus is implemented in `stats.ts` yet — both exist only in the
codex blurb. Until they are, the written power is ahead of the sim.
Flag for the next combat-systems pass.

### Fairness / "makes sense"

- **Defender has no raid-time agency** (Clash-style); that's the
  designed loop. Fine.
- **Unit deployment appears free** (no per-deploy cost in
  `RaidScene.DECK`). Count limits bound the swarm but nothing
  punishes throwing everyone immediately. Consider adding a pheromone
  budget (regenerates over time) if matches start feeling like
  instant all-ins.
- **Bot base `faction: 'Beetles'` uses an all-Ants building roster**
  in `RaidScene.ts`. Cosmetically odd — either give the bot a true
  beetle kit (visually differentiated buildings), or set
  `faction: 'Ants'` to reflect what's on the board. The lore framing
  above ("rival beetle colony") argues for the former eventually.
- **Loot vs. HP match-up** — a SugarVault drops 500 sugar on destroy
  vs. its 350 HP. A Forager can take that with a small escort. Feels
  right for a 90-second raid.

### Suggested follow-ups

1. Implement ShieldBeetle's 60% aura in `combat.ts`.
2. Implement Ambusher first-strike-triple and Jumper wall-hop.
3. Decide whether those two need stat cuts once the bonuses land.
4. Reconcile `RaidScene.BOT_BASE.faction` with its building set.
5. Consider a pheromone budget / deploy cost.

None of these block today's playability — the raid is winnable and the
codex reads cleanly — but they're the cracks to patch before combat
depth can grow further.
